  \begin{abstract}
  In stem cell transplantation, registered potential donors may refuse to donate when matched with patients, causing supply shortages. Thus, preventing such donor dropout is crucial. To address this issue, we developed a behavioral intervention comprising adding messages into the letters sent when donors are matched with patients \revise{and} conducted a field experiment to verify the effect of our intervention. The results demonstrate that our behavioral interventions can prevent registered donor dropout and increase donor availability for transplantation. Specifically, the message emphasizing the limited number of compatible donors per patient increased the probability of donors completing the confirmatory typing stage by \revise{$7.3$}\%. Since physicians can choose the best donor for proceeding to donation from those who complete the confirmatory typing stage, this message effect increases donor availability for transplantation.

  \vspace{0.5em}

  \noindent
  \textit{Keywords}: prosocial behavior, field experiment, free-riding, information provision, Japan Marrow Donor Program, stem cell transplantation

  \vspace{0.5em}

  \noindent
  \textit{JEL classification}: D64, D90, H41, I11
  \end{abstract}

\hypertarget{intro}{%
\section{Introduction}\label{intro}}

Similar to organ transplantation, allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) faces supply shortages in many countries. To resolve this issue, particularly in the context of organ transplantation from deceased donors, many studies have examined interventions to increase donor registrations \citep[for example,][]{Kessler2012}. In addition, HSCT faces another challenge: registered donors may refuse to donate when matched with patients \citep[for example,][]{Switzer2018, Haylock2024}. Thus, developing interventions to mitigate such donor dropout is crucial. This study examines how behavioral interventions at the time of matching can prevent donor dropout and resolve the supply shortage issue.

HSCT has the lowest relapse rate among all treatments for leukemia and other blood cancers. In this treatment, (1) anticancer drugs or radiation are used to kill tumor cells and (2) healthy hematopoietic stem cells are transplanted from a donor. The transplantation requires that the donor's white blood cell type (HLA) completely or partially matches the patient's HLA.\footnote{When HLA-identical or partially matched unrelated donors are unavailable, several options exist. The first option is haploidentical stem cell transplantation in which stem cells are transplanted from a close relative with semi-matched HLA. The second option is cord blood transplantation in which stem cells are transplanted from the umbilical cord or placenta of pregnant women. The HLA requirements for these transplants are weaker than those for bone marrow (or peripheral blood stem cell (PBSC)) transplants between unrelated individuals. Notably, in Japan, bone marrow and PBSC transplants between unrelated individuals accounted for 20\% of all transplants in 2021 \citep{JDCHCT2022}.} When patients cannot find a compatible donor among their relatives, who are more likely to be matched, they can search for unrelated donors through \revise{donor registries such as National Marrow Donor Program (NMDP) and Japan Marrow Donor Program (JMDP).}\footnote{While the probability of a complete match between two randomly selected individuals is less than 1\%, the probability of a full match between siblings is 30\%.}

\revise{However, donor registries worldwide face a common challenge: dropout among registered donors \citep{Switzer2004, Lown2014, Hamed2023}. Donor candidates matched with patients undergo confirmatory typing (CT) to assess their eligibility for transplantation. Because physicians select the most suitable donor based on CT results, dropout before CT reduces donor availability. For example, dropout at the CT stage is 23\% in DKMS Germany \citep{Haylock2024}, and} 56\% of transplant coordinations in JMDP do not reach the CT stage for donor-related reasons \citep{Hirakawa2018}. \revise{Donor dropout prolongs time to transplantation and worsens patient outcomes \citep{Lown2013}.}\footnote{In the JMDP, between 2004 and 2013, only 60\% of the patients registered with the JMDP end up receiving transplantation. Moreover, 58\% of patients who died (or had their transplantation interrupted because of poor health) passed away within 200 days \citep{Hirakawa2018}.} Thus, \revise{interventions to reduce pre-CT donor dropout are needed while preserving individual autonomy.}\footnote{\revise{One may think that expanding donor pool (i.e., recruiting new donors) is one solution. However, many registries have already undertaken such activities for a long time, so the marginal benefit of expanding the donor pool is limited.} Indeed, in the JMDP, the benefit of expanding the donor pool is limited because most patients do eventually find donor candidates \citep{Takanashi2016}.}

In this study, we developed information interventions to increase the number of donor candidates who progress to the CT stage, in collaboration with the JMDP. The interventions added messages to the letters sent when donors are matched with patients. This letter informs \revise{donors of their compatibility with a specific patient} and requests their participation in the transplantation coordination. Upon responding to this so-called ``HLA match letter,'' matched donors indicate their willingness to donate and proceed to the CT stage. We developed two intervention messages---\emph{matching difficulty} and \emph{early coordination}. \revise{The former emphasizes the limited number of HLA-compatible donors per patient to prevent motivational crowding-out of altruistic donors. The latter emphasizes the importance of early coordination for patient outcomes to prevent delayed response to the HLA match letter.} Our experimental participants comprised recipients of the 11,154 HLA match letters that were sent between September 2021 and February 2022.

Using coordination data provided by the JMDP, we \revise{found} that our intervention increases the probability of donors reaching the CT stage. Specifically, \revise{the matching difficulty message increased the probability of reaching CT by $7.3$\%, whereas the early coordination message did not have the intended effect. Although we did not conduct a pilot study or preregister the experiment, these findings are robust to several checks, including covariate adjustment, multiple-hypothesis testing across treatment groups, and logistic regression. Taken together, our field experiment suggests that perceived scarcity of compatible donors prevents motivational crowding-out of altruistic donors.}

\revise{We contribute to the literature on information provision in donor engagement. The key distinction with previous studies lies in both the intervention objective and the target population. Previous studies have mainly aimed to increase total supply and targeted the general population, including individuals who have not registered as donors (i.e., individuals with lower willingness to donate) \citep{Li2016, Sallis2018, Robitaille2021, Goette2020, Goette2024}.}\footnote{\citet{Heger2020} showed that establishing registries that collect potential donors beforehand is effective in increasing the supply of blood donation. In the case of HSCT, such registries already exist. Our motivation is to prevent dropout among registered donors when they are matched with patients.} \revise{In contrast, our objective is to prevent dropout among registered donors rather than to increase the total supply. Accordingly, our target population consists of registered donors, who are more likely to have altruistic preferences \citep{Bergstrom2009, Ohtake2020}.}\footnote{Previous studies have also examined the effects of behavioral interventions such as message and information provision in health and medical policy contexts, including vaccination \citep[for example,][]{Dai2021, Milkman2021}, breast cancer screening \citep{Bertoni2020}, and cord blood transplantation \citep{Grieco2018}. Unlike our study, these studies targeted the general population \revise{including peoplw with low motivation to take action}.}

\revise{However, a few studies share the same intervention goal as ours. \citet{Switzer2018} examined information provision interventions at the time of matching, but their study was not a randomized controlled trial. \citet{Haylock2024} examined the effects of commitment before matching, and thus the timing of their intervention differs from ours. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to document the effects of information provision at the time of matching among an altruistic population through a randomized controlled trial.}

\revise{We also contribute to the literature on social norm messages by demonstrating the effectiveness of a message that is contrasted with social norm messages. Social norm messages describe majority behavior and can induce social image concerns and conformity \citep{Bernheim1994, Bursztyn2017}. Their effectiveness has been shown in the context of monetary donations \citep{Shang2009, Kessler2017}. By contrast, our message emphasizes that only a small number of people can act. In standard economic theory of public goods, information that many others have already acted may discourage individual behavior. In the vaccination context, such information reduces individuals' willingness to be vaccinated \citep{Lau2019, Galizzi2022}. Consistent with these findings, our experiment shows that scarcity information is effective in an altruistic population.}\footnote{\citet{Brekke2011} showed that free-riding in public goods provision is eliminated when altruistic groups are formed through individual choice. \revise{This result is consistent with our finding.}}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section \ref{experiment} provides an overview of the coordination process of the JMDP and describes our field experiment. Section \ref{result} presents the results of our experiment. Section \ref{discussion} discusses the welfare implications. Finally, Section \ref{conclusion} concludes.

\hypertarget{background}{%
\subsection{Background: Coordination Process of the JMDP}\label{background}}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics{image/flowchart} \caption{Flowchart of the JMDP Coordination Process and Sample Selection \newline \emph{Note}: The numbers in this figure are based on our experimental data.}\label{fig:flow-chart-coordination}
\end{figure}

To allow readers to better understand our intervention and data, we first outline the coordination process leading to stem cell donation through the JMDP (see Figure \ref{fig:flow-chart-coordination} for a visual overview). Individuals eligible to register as donors with the JMDP must be between 18 and 54 years and in good health.\footnote{Additionally, men must weigh at least 45 kg and women must weigh at least 40 kg. See \url{https://www.jmdp.or.jp/reg/requirement/} (Japanese; Accessed October 24, 2025).} Individuals who can actually donate must be between 20 and 55 years. As of December 2024, approximately 560,000 donors were registered with the JMDP. Of these, 240,000 (42\%) were under 40 years and 280,000 (50\%) were male.\footnote{Data source: \url{https://www.bs.jrc.or.jp/bmdc/donorregistrant/m2_03_00_statistics.html} (Japanese; Accessed October 24, 2025).}

After a patient's physician registers the patient with the JMDP, the JMDP compiles a list of candidates based on the physician's criteria (primarily HLA compatibility). The physician selects up to 10 matched donors from this list. Subsequently, the JMDP office sends each matched donor an HLA match letter, recommending a response within seven days.\footnote{Simultaneously, the JMDP office sends a short message to matched donors informing them that the JMDP has sent the HLA match letter.} Our experimental participants are all matched donors who received HLA match letters between September 2021 and February 2022. Upon responding, matched donors complete a medical questionnaire and indicate their willingness to donate.

A matched and willing donor undergoes CT within approximately one month. CT confirms whether the matched donor meets the criteria for transplantation set by the JMDP. The transplantation coordinator delegated by the JMDP explains the details of the donation process and asks matched donors and their families about their willingness to donate. Thereafter, the coordinating physician conducts medical interviews, examinations, and blood tests, including those for infectious disease markers. Collection methods (bone marrow or PBSC collection) are also determined based on the preferences of the matched donor and coordinating physician. CT can be skipped when a matched donor is eligible based on a previous CT result within one year.

The patient's physician selects the best donor from matched donors who have reached CT. When the JMDP determines that a matched donor is eligible based on their CT results, the physician receives those results and must classify the donor into three categories within 40 days: best donor, backup donor, or rejection. The physician selects only the best donor to proceed to final consent and donation but can select up to four backup donors, who are ranked by priority. When coordination with the best donor is interrupted, the highest-priority backup donor becomes the best donor.\footnote{Even after selecting the best donor, if the JMDP determines that additional matched donors are eligible, the physician must classify them as backup donors or rejections within 40 days. However, if the best donor has not yet provided final consent, the physician can change the best donor to a later-arriving matched donor.}

The best donor must provide final consent after being informed of their selection by the coordinator and coordinating physician. Simultaneously, a representative of the donor's family must provide consent to the donation. After final consent is obtained, the donor cannot withdraw their decision to donate. They then undergo a surgical procedure to collect stem cells. The time from CT to collection is approximately three to four months. Importantly, throughout the coordination process, a matched donor cannot access any information about the matched patient (e.g., the number of other available potential donors), nor can the matched donor obtain this information from the transplantation coordinator or coordinating physician.

Our intervention involved adding two messages into the HLA match letter.\footnote{In designing our intervention messages, we were careful to avoid placing undue psychological pressure on potential donors. Specifically, we avoided using language that sounded like an appeal. Additionally, we only used information publicly available from the JMDP. Finally, the risks of transplantation were explained in a typical manner. The intervention message was approved by the institutional review boards of the Graduate School of Economics, Osaka University and the JMDP.} Table \ref{tab:list-message} lists the content of the standard letter and the two intervention messages. The JMDP also encloses a handbook that describes the coordination process as well as the medical questionnaire and donor consent form.\footnote{The handbook can be found at \url{https://www.jmdp.or.jp/pdf/en/DonorHandBook201708.pdf} (Accessed December 9, 2024).} We developed two messages based on the finding from our preliminary survey that the target population for our intervention includes more altruistic individuals than the general population \citep{Ohtake2020}.

The matching difficulty message emphasizes the low number of matched donors per registered patient. If other potential donors with the same HLA type are in the pool, one donor's donation can be substituted with that of another compatible donor. Additionally, multiple matched donors (up to 10) can be simultaneously matched with a single patient. Therefore, transplantation through the JMDP represents a public good \citep{Bergstrom2009}. \revise{In this setting, self-interested individuals who do not care about the patient do not contribute, regardless of the presence of substitutable donors. By contrast, individuals with altruistic preferences exhibit two types of behavior. First, purely altruistic individuals who care about transplant outcome may crowd out their motivation by donation from substitutable donors.}\footnote{\citet{Ohtake2020} found that 22\% of registrants had pure altruistic preferences. Moreover, \citet{Kurosawa2022} interviewed previously matched donors and found that those with low donation intention felt that they were ``one of several donors.'' This implies that some potential donors with low intention recognize that their donation could be substituted by others.} \revise{Second, warm-glow individuals who derive utility from the act of giving itself may donate regardless of the presence of substitutable donors} \citep{Andreoni1990}. Thus, the matching difficulty message emphasizes the small number of possible substitutes and is expected to \revise{prevent motivational crowding-out} of purely altruistic individuals and increase their willingness to donate.\footnote{There are other two mechanisms. First, since matched donors cannot know the number of substitutable donors, this uncertainty may reduce their willingness to donate \citep{Exley2016}. The matching difficulty message reduces this uncertainty. Second, the message makes the rarity of the opportunity to donate more salient, which may change donor behavior.}

The early coordination message emphasizes that early coordination would increase the patient's transplantation rate. Our preliminary survey found that registered donors who place higher value on immediate outcomes (higher time discount rates) were more likely to proceed with donations \citep{Ohtake2020}. Motivated by this finding, we created a message that emphasized the value of responding now, rather than postponing the response. Conversely, this message increases the perceived cost of refusing to donate \citep{Haylock2024}.\footnote{\citet{Haylock2024} showed that notifying registries in advance of the period during which donation is possible functions as a commitment and increases the perceived cost of refusing to donate.} Furthermore, earlier responses are associated with higher rates of reaching the CT stage (Figure \ref{fig:speed-CT-cond-response}). Therefore, this message is expected to encourage early responses and increase the probability of donors undergoing CT.

Four experimental groups were established to estimate the effects of the two intervention messages. The first group received the standard HLA match letter with neither of the intervention messages, serving as the control group (StatusQuo group). The second and third groups received the standard HLA match letter including only the matching difficulty and early coordination messages, respectively (MatchMessage and CoordMessage groups, respectively). The last group received the standard HLA match letter with both intervention messages included to test the negative effect of information overload (BothMessage group).

The experimental participants consisted of the recipients of the 11,154 letters sent out to matched donors between September 6, 2021 and February 27, 2022.\footnote{In a few cases during our experimental period, the same registrant matched with more than one patient and received letters multiple times.} To maintain randomness to the best of the JMDP office's abilities, we assigned letters to the four experimental groups using weekly cluster randomization. Specifically, we created a cluster every seven days (a week starting from Monday) and randomly assigned one experimental group to each cluster (see Table \ref{tab:assignment}). The assignment of the experimental groups was designed to be as balanced across weeks and months as possible.

Before conducting the experiment, we obtained approval from the institutional review boards of the Graduate School of Economics, Osaka University (approval number: R030305) and the JMDP (approval number: JMDP2021-04) for the experimental design and empirical strategy. We did not conduct pilot studies and did not register the experiment with a pre-analysis plan.

\hypertarget{data}{%
\subsection{Data and Empirical Strategy}\label{data}}

In the coordination data provided by the JMDP, the unit of observation was a coordination. The data included donor characteristics such as sex, age, number of coordination experiences, and prefecture-level residential area. The data on the coordination process included whether each coordination stage (response to the letter, CT, donor selection, final consent, and collection) had been completed. Additionally, the data included the number of days taken to respond to the HLA match letter and willingness to donate at the time of responding to the letter. If the coordination process was interrupted before donation, the reasons for the interruption were recorded in three categories: patient-side reasons, donor non-health reasons, and donor health reasons. Unfortunately, the data included no information on patients such as their characteristics or prognosis.

As additional data, we used the list of medical institutions published on the JMDP website.\footnote{Data source: \url{https://www.jmdp.or.jp/hospitals/view2/} (Accessed August 4, 2022).} This list includes complete addresses, the availability of bone marrow collection, and the availability of PBSC collection. We aggregated this list at the prefecture level and calculated the number of hospitals per 10 square kilometers.\footnote{To calculate the density of the hospitals in each prefecture, we used the land area data provided by the Geospatial Information Authority of Japan.} This aggregated dataset was merged with the coordination data using the prefecture as the merge key. We considered this variable as the traveling cost of coordination and donation.

The analysis sample included 11,049 completed or interrupted coordinations involving matched donors living in Japan.\footnote{One coordination involved a matched donor who lived abroad. There were 104 coordinations with ongoing coordination at the time of data collection. The proportion of ongoing coordinations was balanced across the experimental groups (F-test, p-value = \(0.383\)).} Of these coordinations, \(4.6\)\% were completed (donation was made) and \(63.5\)\% involved matched donors coordinating for the first time. The mean and median age of matched donors were 39 and \(37.98\) years, respectively, while \(62.4\)\% of coordinations involved male donors. There were slightly more young donors and male donors than in the donor pool.\footnote{Regarding geographic distribution, \(17.1\)\% of coordinations involved matched donors who lived in Tokyo or Osaka, where hospital density is well above the national average (\(1.89\) and \(1.36\), respectively compared with the national average of \(0.21\)).}

The primary outcome was whether matched donors underwent CT. This outcome is important for two reasons. First, reaching the CT stage requires donors to respond to the HLA match letter, indicate their willingness to donate, and maintain their intentions. Additionally, late responses lead to coordination interruptions before the CT stage. Second, physicians can select the most appropriate donor for transplantation from among the matched donors who have undergone CT. Thus, showing up for CT represents both a costly prosocial behavior and an important practical indicator of coordination.

In the StatusQuo group, only \(22.25\)\% of matched donors underwent CT. Two factors explain this low rate. First, only \(54.91\)\% indicated their willingness to donate. Second, \(59.48\)\% of those who expressed a willingness to donate did not reach the CT stage (i.e., dropped out between the response and CT), and \(85.99\)\% of these dropouts were donor-related reasons. According to \citet{Hirakawa2018}, \revise{the top donor-related reasons are health conditions such as lower back pain, scheduling conflicts, and the lack of family consent.}\footnote{Using values from \citet{Hirakawa2018}, \(59.33\)\% of dropouts was due to donor health conditions, \(13.76\)\% of dropouts was due to scheduling conflicts, and \(8.60\)\% of dropouts was due to the lack of family consent.} \revise{Thus, major obstacles are low willingness to donate and donor-related dropout between the response and CT.}

Table \ref{tab:summary} summarizes our experiment. Panels A and B present the intervention and sample size for each experimental group, respectively. Panel C presents a balance test of whether the randomization was successful. There were statistically significant differences in the average age and number of holidays in the assignment week and the following week between the experimental groups. The average age of the CoordMessage group was approximately one year younger than that of the StatusQuo group. However, this difference was negligible from a standardized mean difference perspective (see Table A1 in Supplementary Material A). The average number of holidays for the MatchMessage group was higher than that of the StatusQuo group. This occurred because for a cluster of the MatchMessage group assigned in December, the week following their assignment week included the New Year's holiday (see Table \ref{tab:assignment}).\footnote{In Japan, the New Year's holiday is set by the government to run from December 29 to January 3. While we follow this definition, this holiday may be extended depending on individual circumstances.}

Although most covariates were balanced across the experimental groups, a few were unbalanced.\footnote{We confirmed that age and the number of holidays did not affect CT, which is our primary outcome, in the StatusQuo group. Therefore, the imbalance in some covariates is not a major concern. We present the results of a regression analysis that adjusts for these effects in Supplementary Material A.} Therefore, in addition to a simple difference-in-means test, we estimated the following linear regression model for HLA match letter \(i\) sent out in week \(w\) of month \(m\):

\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
  Y_{iwm} =& \alpha + \beta_1 \cdot \text{MatchMessage}_{wm}
  + \beta_2 \cdot \text{CoordMessage}_{wm} + \beta_3 \cdot \text{BothMessage}_{wm} \\
  &+ X'_{iwm} \gamma + u_{iwm},
  \end{split}\label{eq:reg}
\end{equation}

\noindent
where \(X_{iwm}\) is a vector of observable characteristics, including donor age and the number of holidays. The parameters of interest are \((\beta_1, \beta_2, \beta_3)\).

\revise{There may be within-patient correlation because multiple donors can be matched with the same patient and physicians select one from among those who reach CT. Ideally, we would cluster standard errors at the patient level (or two-way by patient and assigned week). Unfortunately, we do not have patient IDs. Instead, we clustered standard errors by the assigned week, the level of treatment assignment. This may also partially capture patient-level correlation when donors matched with the same patient received letters in the same week.}

As mentioned previously, our intervention is expected to increase the probability of reaching CT by improving the willingness to donate and preventing dropout between the response and CT stages. Therefore, we decomposed the intervention message effects into two components: responses with a willingness to donate and prevention of dropout for donor-side reasons (endogenous dropout). The results are presented in Table \ref{tab:lm-test-decompose}. Although the evidence is suggestive, our intervention worked as expected, except for the CoordMessage group.

However, the mechanisms differed slightly across the intervention groups. The willingness to donate increased primarily in the MatchMessage group. As shown in column (1), the willingness to donate in this group increased by \(2.31\) percentage points, statistically significant at the 10\% level. Endogenous dropout in the MatchMessage group also fell by approximately 1 percentage point, although this was not statistically significant. Combining these effects, the probability of reaching CT increased in the MatchMessage group. By contrast, the CoordMessage and BothMessage groups primarily contributed to preventing endogenous dropout. In particular, as shown in column (4), endogenous dropout fell by \(2.27\) percentage points in the BothMessage group, statistically significant at the 5\% level. However, the willingness to donate of these groups did not sufficiently increase, limiting the increase in the probability of reaching CT in both groups. Therefore, these findings suggest that the matching difficulty message may help improve the willingness to donate, while the early coordination message may prevent endogenous dropout.

\hypertarget{discussion}{%
\section{Policy and Welfare Implications of the Intervention}\label{discussion}}

To discuss the policy and welfare implications of the intervention, we examine treatment effects on the post-CT stages: donor selection, final consent, and collection (donation). These stages are influenced by both our intervention and patient-side demand. As a benchmark, when the JMDP sends HLA match letters to 10 donors and all reach the CT stage, selection and donation rates reach a maximum of 10\% because a physician selects one best donor.\footnote{When desirable donors are not found through the CT stage or when few matched donors complete the CT stage, a physician may be unable to find the best donor among matched donors. In this case, selection and donation rates fall below 10\%. Conversely, when the JMDP office sends HLA match letters to fewer than 10 matched donors, selection and donation rates exceed 10\%.} In the StatusQuo group, the selection and donation rates are \(6.19\)\% and \(4.5\)\%, respectively; the gap reflects dropout due to patient deterioration or failure to obtain final consent.

Our intervention had no statistically significant effects on post-CT outcomes (Figure A2 and Table A3 in Supplementary Material A). The estimated effects on all three stages are smaller than on the CT stage: donor selection and donation in the MatchMessage group fell by \(0.29\) and \(0.12\) percentage points, respectively, which were not statistically significant.\footnote{Logistic regression yielded similar results (see Table A4 in Supplementary Material A).} In sum, the matching difficulty message promoted reaching the CT stage but did not increase selection or donation rates.

We should not conclude from this result alone that patient welfare did not improve. Because more matched donors reached CT in the MatchMessage group, physicians could choose the best donor from a larger pool. The intervention may thus raise transplant quality (e.g., better donor selection and patient prognosis) even if it does not raise quantity.

Heterogeneous treatment effects by gender, age, and past coordination experience support this possibility (Figure A3 in Supplementary Material A). The MatchMessage treatment encouraged young men with past coordination experience to reach CT (7 percentage points; \(p = 0.05\)) and increased their probability of being selected as the best donor (5 percentage points; \(p < 0.01\)). This result is desirable given evidence that young male donors improve patient prognosis \citep{Loren2006, Arai2016, Kollman2016, Shinohara2017}.\footnote{Using those in the StatusQuo group who reached the CT stage, we examine which characteristics make candidates more likely to be selected as the best donor (Table A5 in Supplementary Material A). As a result, young men were more likely to be selected as the best donor, consistent with medical findings. We also find that donor selection and donation were unrelated to the speed of reaching the CT stage (those who responded to the HLA match letter quickly or could skip CT).} Thus, the MatchMessage treatment may improve transplant quality by encouraging such donors to reach CT.

At the same time, the MatchMessage treatment promoted older men matched for the first time and young women with past coordination experience to reach CT, but did not increase their probability of selection.\footnote{The MatchMessage group changed the behavior of both those likely to be selected as donors and those unlikely to be selected, resulting in average treatment effects on donor selection and donation close to zero. This reflects the result that the MatchMessage treatment increased coordinations interrupted for exogenous reasons (donor health reasons such as disqualification because of the CT results and patient-side reasons) among those who reached the CT stage (Table A6 in Supplementary Material A).} They may thus have wasted their time, and the intervention may have reduced donor welfare in this sense. Therefore, interventions targeting young men may be more appropriate than a uniform message to all matched donors, from the perspectives of welfare and cost-effectiveness.

\hypertarget{conclusion}{%
\section{Conclusions}\label{conclusion}}

This study examined how information provision at the time of matching affects donor availability in stem cell transplantation. \revise{Our} field experiment showed that providing information on the limited number of HLA-compatible donors per patient (i.e., the matching difficulty message) increased the probability of reaching CT, \revise{expanding the pool from which physicians can select the best donor. However, this effect was attenuated when the message was presented together with the ineffective early coordination message, possibly due to information overload. Although we did not conduct a pilot study or preregister the protocol and pre-analysis plan, our findings are robust to several checks, including covariate adjustment, multiple-hypothesis testing across treatment groups, and logistic regression. These results suggest that effective information should be conveyed in a simple manner.}

\revise{To evaluate the cost-effectiveness, we translate the effect of the matching difficulty message into an equivalent number of additional donor registrations. After adjusting for covariates and month fixed effects, the message increased the CT completion rate by $1.63$ percentage points. Given the baseline CT completion rate of $22.25$\% in the StatusQuo group, this implies a $7.3\% (= 1.63/22.25)$ increase, equivalent to a $7.3$\% increase in matched donors.}\footnote{Let \(N_m\) be the number of registrants who start the coordination process. Let \(N_1(d)\) be the number of potential donors who reach CT under treatment \(d\). Then, \(N_1(d) = p(d)N_m\) holds, where \(p(d)\) is the probability of reaching CT under treatment \(d\). Note that \(d = 1\) and \(d = 0\) represent the treatment and control groups, respectively. We solve \(N_1(1) = p(0)[N_m + \Delta N_m]\) for \(\Delta N_m\), where \(\Delta N_m\) represents the increase in registrants who start coordination. Therefore, we obtain \([p(1) - p(0)]/p(0) = \Delta N_m/N_m\).} \revise{Since 40\% of registrants become matched donors, $224,000$ of the current $560,000$ registrants are potential matched donors.}\footnote{The cumulative number of registrants since the JMDP's establishment is \(980,000\). Among these registrants, \(390,000\) have become potential donors who started the coordination process. Therefore, 40\% of registrants become potential donors who start coordination. Data source: \url{https://www.bs.jrc.or.jp/bmdc/donorregistrant/m2_03_00_statistics.html} (Accessed December 8, 2024).} \revise{Therefore, the message effect implies an increase of $16,352$ ($= 224,000 \times 0.073$) in matched donors, or an increase of $40,880$ ($= 560,000 \times 0.073$) in registrants.}

\revise{This equivalence has direct policy relevance. In the current JMDP donor pool, $100,000$ donors in their 50s will exit within the next five years because of the age limit for donation (54 years).}\footnote{\url{https://www.jmdp.or.jp/about/read/number/} (Accessed December 8, 2024).} \revise{Since the message effect is equivalent to recruiting $40,880$ new donors, the matching difficulty message can therefore offset approximately $40.9\%$ ($= 40,880/100,000$) of the negative impact of this donor-pool shrinkage.}

\revise{Meanwhile, donor recruitment is costly. While the cost required to add one donor registration at the JMDP is unknown, NMDP spends approximately USD 150 per added registration.}\footnote{\url{https://fundraise.nmdp.org/index.cfm?fuseaction=cms.page\&id=1203\&eventID=675} (Accessed March 29, 2025).} \revise{If the JMDP bears a similar cost, recruiting $40,880$ registrants would cost approximately USD $6.132$ million. Thus, adding the matching difficulty message could be a more cost-effective measure than recruiting new donors to address the shrinking donor pool.}

Finally, \revise{our intervention may apply to contexts beyond stem cell transplantation. The matching difficulty message emphasizes the scarcity of potential cooperators in public-goods settings. In our context, it may have corrected donors' overestimation of avairable substitutes and reduced motivational crowding-out. Alternatively, it may have made the rarity of cooperative opportunities more salient. Although our data cannot identify the dominant mechanism, communicating scarcity is likely to be effective in other cooperative settings such as rare-blood donation.}
